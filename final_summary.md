# 1. Overview of evidence

The collected studies provide insights into the roles of dendritic spines in CA1 pyramidal neurons of the hippocampus, focusing on their morphology, density, regulation mechanisms, and functional consequences on synaptic plasticity and cognition. The research highlights molecular regulators such as autophagy, EFA6A-Arf6 signaling, MARK1, and developmental influences including fetal alcohol exposure and the presence of Cajal-Retzius cells. These factors collectively affect dendritic spine morphology (size and subtype distribution), density, and the associated synaptic plasticity, which underpin learning and memory processes. Common observations include alterations in spine density and morphology leading to cognitive impairments, demonstrating the critical role of spine regulation in hippocampal function.

# 2. Key mechanisms / findings

- **Autophagy and caspase-3 regulation:** Autophagy, particularly mediated by ATG5, regulates dendritic spine density and volume differently between proximal and distal CA1 dendrites. Caspase-3 influences spine volume and morphology but not density (PMID: 41546430).
- **Cytoskeletal dynamics:** Fetal alcohol exposure reduces dendritic arborization and spine density in CA1 neurons, along with decreased polymerized F-actin, implicating disrupted actin cytoskeleton dynamics in spine deficits and cognitive impairments (PMID: 40345553).
- **EFA6A-Arf6 signaling:** EFA6A isoforms regulate dendritic spine density bidirectionally during development, affecting synaptic plasticity (LTP) and hippocampus-dependent memory in vivo (PMID: 40335791).
- **MARK1 kinase function:** MARK1 deletion reduces spine density and alters synaptic protein localization in CA1 neurons, leading to spatial learning deficits and changes in anxiety-like behavior (PMID: 38484863).
- **Cajal-Retzius cell influence:** Postnatal persistence of Cajal-Retzius cells is critical for normal dendritic complexity and spine density in CA1 pyramidal neurons and influences synaptic gene expression and network formation (PMID: 38095282).

# 3. Consensus in the literature

- Dendritic spine morphology and density in CA1 pyramidal neurons are essential for synaptic plasticity, learning, and memory.
- Molecular pathways regulating actin cytoskeleton dynamics (e.g., EFA6A-Arf6, autophagy, MARK1) strongly influence spine formation, maintenance, and morphology.
- Alterations in dendritic spine density and morphology correlate with cognitive impairments, as shown in models of fetal alcohol exposure and genetic modifications.
- Both intrinsic neuronal mechanisms and extrinsic developmental factors (like Cajal-Retzius cells) contribute to the establishment and plasticity of dendritic spines in CA1 neurons.

# 4. Limitations of current research

- Many studies rely on animal models (mostly mice), limiting direct translational inference to humans.
- Sex differences were assessed only in some studies (e.g., fetal alcohol exposure), potentially overlooking critical variations.
- The mechanistic links between altered spine morphology/density and precise cognitive phenotypes remain correlational rather than causative in many cases.
- The dynamics of dendritic spine regulation in different CA1 dendritic compartments are incompletely characterized, especially in physiological conditions.
- Some molecular pathways, such as autophagy’s distinct role in proximal vs distal dendrites, require further replication and functional validation.
- Longitudinal studies addressing developmental changes across the lifespan are limited.

# 5. Research gaps and future directions

- Detailed mechanistic studies dissecting how autophagy differentially regulates proximal and distal dendritic spines at the molecular level.
- Clarification of how specific spine subtypes (thin, stubby, mushroom) functionally contribute to hippocampal circuit plasticity and behavior.
- Exploration of sex-specific effects on dendritic spine morphology and related cognitive functions.
- Investigations into interactions between developmental environmental insults (e.g., ethanol exposure) and molecular regulators of spine plasticity.
- Functional studies linking spine molecular alterations with defined cognitive and behavioral outcomes in awake, behaving animals.
- Longitudinal analyses to track changes in spine morphology and function across developmental stages and aging.
- Integration of transcriptomic and proteomic data with high-resolution spine morphological analyses to identify novel regulatory molecules.

# 6. Conclusion

Dendritic spines in CA1 pyramidal neurons serve as critical substrates for synaptic plasticity and cognitive function, regulated by multiple molecular pathways including autophagy, cytoskeletal remodeling, and signaling proteins like EFA6A and MARK1. Both intrinsic cellular mechanisms and extrinsic developmental factors shape their morphology and density, with disruptions linked to cognitive deficits. While these studies provide foundational knowledge, further detailed and longitudinal research is needed to fully elucidate the nuanced regulation of spines and translate findings toward therapeutic strategies.

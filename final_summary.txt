# 1. Overview of evidence

The selected studies collectively highlight the critical role of dendritic spines in CA1 pyramidal neurons for hippocampal function, particularly related to synaptic plasticity, learning, and memory. Dendritic spine morphology and density are shown to be dynamically regulated by various molecular pathways, including autophagy, ion channels (Ttyh1), guanine nucleotide exchange factors (EFA6A), ferroptosis regulators (CISD2), and neuroprotective cytokines (G-CSF). These factors impact spine volume, shape, density, and consequently influence synaptic efficacy and cognitive outcomes in both normal development and pathological conditions such as brain ischemia and heatstroke. The results underscore that alterations in dendritic spines can underlie impairments in synaptic plasticity and memory performance.

# 2. Key mechanisms / findings

- **Autophagy** regulates dendritic spine volume and morphology differentially in proximal vs. distal dendrites of CA1 neurons; it also reduces spine density when disrupted (PMID:41546430).
- **Tweety-homolog 1 (Ttyh1)** modulates dendritic complexity and spine morphology and density in CA1 and CA3 pyramidal neurons, suggesting its role in neuronal structural plasticity (PMID:40816602).
- **CISD2** protects against heatstroke-induced early cognitive deficits by inhibiting ferroptosis, preserving dendritic spine density in CA1 neurons, and maintaining hippocampal function under stress (PMID:40398723).
- **EFA6A** regulates spine morphogenesis in a developmental stage-dependent manner with reduced spine density during development and aberrant increases in adulthood, impacting long-term potentiation and memory retention (PMID:40335791).
- **Granulocyte colony-stimulating factor (G-CSF)** ameliorates memory impairments after brain ischemia by improving dendritic length, arborization, spine density, and maturity in CA1 pyramidal neurons (PMID:37728699).

# 3. Consensus in the literature

- Most studies agree that dendritic spine morphology and density in CA1 pyramidal neurons are critical substrates for synaptic plasticity and cognitive functions including learning and memory.
- Molecular regulators modulating spine volume, shape, and density (e.g., autophagy, EFA6A signaling) are essential for maintaining normal hippocampal function.
- Pathological insults such as ischemia or heatstroke negatively affect dendritic spines, correlating with cognitive deficits.
- Interventions that preserve or restore dendritic spine integrity can improve cognitive outcomes in disease models.

# 4. Limitations of current research

- Many studies use knockout or overexpression models that may not fully replicate physiological conditions.
- In vitro or ex vivo systems, such as organotypic slice cultures, may not completely preserve complex in vivo environments.
- Some findings, especially on spine subtype dynamics and compartment-specific regulation (proximal vs distal), rely on limited imaging methods and reconstructions that might miss fine details.
- Longitudinal studies on spine changes and their direct linkage to behavioral outcomes are limited, reducing causal interpretation confidence.
- Studies focus primarily on rodent models; translational relevance to humans is uncertain without further validation.

# 5. Research gaps and future directions

- The precise molecular signaling cascades linking regulators like autophagy and Ttyh1 to spine subtype-specific changes across dendritic compartments require elucidation.
- More in vivo studies combining longitudinal spine imaging with behavioral assessments are needed to better establish causal relationships.
- Exploration of how systemic factors (e.g., inflammation, metabolic changes) influence spine regulation in CA1 neurons during different pathological states is lacking.
- Investigating potential therapeutic windows and dosing strategies for molecules like G-CSF or CISD2 modulators in clinical contexts remains to be done.
- Comparative studies addressing species differences in dendritic spine plasticity in CA1 pyramidal neurons would enhance clinical relevance.

# 6. Conclusion

Dendritic spines of CA1 pyramidal cells are finely regulated structures integral to hippocampal synaptic plasticity and memory formation. Evidence highlights multiple molecular mechanisms controlling spine morphology and density under physiological and pathological conditions. Although significant progress has been made in identifying key regulators and their functional consequences, further in vivo and translational research is required to fully understand dendritic spine dynamics and to harness this knowledge for therapeutic interventions addressing cognitive impairments associated with neurological diseases.
